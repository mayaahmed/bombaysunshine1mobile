Chapter 4

A friend in love

Tara's friend Padma had news that she was in love. The news amused Tara. Though the movies and books were always full of love stories, to really get involved in one was not that savvy. To a teenage girl it clearly spelled trouble.

Tara recited solemnly:

"So I turned to the garden of love,
that so many sweet flowers bore,
And I saw it was filled with graves."

"Cynic" Padma scolded.

Tara laughed and said

"That verse is by William Blake. He is the cynic not me. Do we grudge a pretty rose its existence just because it is ephemeral? No. Beautiful things are always good for the soul."

"What is ephemeral?" Padma asked.

"Ephemeral means short lived. Love is known to be short lived."

"I will prove otherwise. I will bet that my romance will endure the test of time."

"There is no empirical evidence to support your claim. But this is one bet I will be glad to lose Padma. You look so happy. Love has made you prettier than before."

Sure enough there was a glow on Padma's face. Tara was a little concerned about Padma. Her parents were very orthodox and Tara did not know how they would react if they knew that Padma was bunking classes and meeting Manoj. Manoj lived in an apartment opposite to Padma's apartment.

"I am not afraid. I do not care for anything except Manoj" Padma said as though in a dream.

Just then Manoj surfaced on the Veranda opposite and Tara left the couple to their new found happiness.

And then one day the inevitable happened. Padma and Manoj belonged to two different communities. These communities were constantly at war and the hate percolated surprisingly even to teenagers. So a group of girls in Padma's class decided to break this little secret to Padma's mother.

And that evening when Padma got back supposedly from college but really from a movie she went with Manoj to, she met with a real angry mother at the door. That was the last anyone saw of Padma. She was locked up in a room and the family did not open the door to anyone. In the morning the father left for work and in the evening the mother would open the door only after hearing his voice. The front door opened to no one. Not to vendors, Not to friends.

Now the curiosity of the building people was intense about this affair. The eyes were glued to that door in intense expectation. People were constantly guessing about what is going on and what is going to happen.

Poor Manoj. He was hardly seventeen years of age. He had no job and there was nothing he could do. He hung around and tried to knock at the front door but everyone knew including him that he could do nothing. One day when everyone was watching a really favorite TV program Padma was shipped away to her native village. When Tara heard of it she felt sad.

"Girls are so much trouble for her parents" said Tara's mother. "See how much sorrow she brought to herself and her family."

"Yes" said Tara. "Manoj's family did not do anything. Manoj was not locked up or shipped to a village. But he was involved as much as Padma in the whole thing."

That night Tara wrote in her diary:

"Padma, you won the bet. Your romance is now a pathological one. The world loves pathological romances. I will surely remember your story now. One goes after happiness but it is sorrow that triumphs. It is misery that leaves a mark and arouses deep interest compared to mild happiness."

Days went by and there was no news of Padma. Now Seema Akka had the habit of hanging her washed saris to dry all over the common veranda. The long saris blocked the view of people who lived in the floor below. It was very annoying and any amount of complaining did not stop Seema Akka from hanging her saris to dry thus. One day Padma's mother got angry and she tied knots on the hanging saris. When Seema Akka saw this she got very angry.

"Who tied knots in my saris?" she shouted.

No one answered.

"Who is the witch who tied knots in my saris?" she growled.

Now Padma's mother did not like someone calling her a witch so she came out and said "I tied the knots."

"Now may I ask what prompted you to do that?" Seema Akka asked.

"My view was blocked."

"Oh ho! So your view was blocked was it? But the movie is over isn't it? What else is left to watch?" Seema Akka asked sarcastically.

Padma's mother was shocked. She knew that Seema Akka was pointing to the affair of her daughter. Her eyes clouded with tears and she went inside her house without a further word. Tara was hanging around with her friends and she saw the whole thing. She did not like what she saw and she decided to act.

"What was this movie you were talking about Seema Akka?" Tara asked.

"Don't you know about the movie that was going on in that house?" Seema Akka asked rolling her eyeballs.

"No, I do not know anything about it. I must have missed something exciting."

Tara then turned to her friend and asked "Do you know anything about the movie Jaya?"

"No, nothing at all" Jaya said catching the drift of Tara's plan.

"Do you know anything Seeta?" asked Tara.

"No, I do not know anything about it either" Seeta said smiling.

Now Seema Akka was a little confused. She thought that this was public knowledge and how come Padma's close friends did not know anything. Was her information wrong?

"I heard that Padma and Manoj had something going on ..." Seema Akka started to say a little confused.

That was the wrong thing to say. There were other ladies around and they did not like what they heard Seema Akka say. Somethings were all understood but never mentioned especially in front of young girls.

"Watch what you are saying you moron" an old lady who overheard the conversation warned. "Just because you have no daughters does not give you a right to malign the names of other people's daughters."

Now Charu who lived in the same floor as Padma and who had been suffering the habit of Seema Akka's hanging saris took this opportunity to speak her mind.

"Padma's mother was right in tying knots on your saris Seema Akka. You hang your saris and make other people suffer. Did you live in a jungle before you came to live here?"

"Where will I hang my saris? On your head?" Seema Akka asked angrily.

"Instead of talking like a mad woman why don't you use your head and figure out where you can hang your saris?" Charu replied. "Or you could always fold your saris so that it does not hang so low and block people's view."

Seema Akka heard nothing but that she was called a mad woman. She got very angry and shouted: "Who are you calling mad woman? No respect for my Grey hair. You are the mad woman here. Mad woman. Mad woman. Mad woman."

At this point Charu shook her head and said "There is no point talking to you."

Charu went inside her house and slammed the door. Seema Akka was fuming with rage. She kept complaining about how she was called a mad woman but she had no audience left. Everyone went inside their house. After some time, Tara decided to visit Padma's mother. As she descended the stairs a lot of questions formed in her head.

What was this society that Padma's mother, her mother, and everyone else she knew was in so much in awe for? Did the people of her building define society? Or did the people of Bombay define society? Society was like the concept of god. Everyone had their own definition. They defined their own fears of society. They also defined their own rules. Society like religion was very personal. Nowhere was this more evident than in that little square building of Bombay.

Padma's mother was sitting at a desk with her head on the table. She was crying quietly. She looked up and saw Tara. Tara did not want to judge. She just gave Padma's mother a hug.
